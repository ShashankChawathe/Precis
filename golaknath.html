Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967)

Citation: 1967 AIR 1643; 1967 SCR (2) 762 [file:41]

Bench: K. Subba Rao C.J., K.N. Wanchoo, M. Hidayatullah, J.C. Shah, S.M. Sikri, R.S. Bachawat, V. Ramaswami, J.M. Shelat, V. Bhargava, G.K. Mitter, C.A. Vaidyialingam JJ. [file:41]

Background:

The petitioners challenged the Punjab Security of Land Tenures Act, 1953 and the Mysore Land Reforms Act, 1962 as amended, arguing that these laws violated fundamental rights. Because both statutes had been placed in the Ninth Schedule by the Constitution (Seventeenth Amendment) Act, 1964, the petitioners also attacked the validity of that constitutional amendment.[file:41]

The core dispute was whether Parliament could amend or abridge the fundamental rights in Part III by using the procedure under Article 368, and whether such an amendment was “law” within the meaning of Article 13(2).[file:41]

Issues:

1. Whether Parliament has power under Article 368 to amend fundamental rights contained in Part III of the Constitution.[file:41]

2. Whether a constitutional amendment is “law” within the meaning of Article 13(2), and therefore invalid if it abridges or takes away fundamental rights.[file:41]

3. Whether the Constitution (Seventeenth Amendment) Act, 1964 was valid, and whether the Ninth Schedule protection given to the impugned agrarian laws could stand.[file:41]

Judgement:

By a majority, the Court held that fundamental rights cannot be abridged or taken away by the amending procedure in Article 368, and that a constitutional amendment is “law” for the purposes of Article 13(2). Therefore, any amendment infringing fundamental rights would be void.[file:41]

The majority overruled earlier decisions in Sankari Prasad and Sajjan Singh, and held that Parliament does not have unlimited power to amend Part III; the rights in Part III have a transcendental position and are kept beyond Parliament’s ordinary amending power.[file:41]

However, the doctrine of prospective overruling was adopted, so the Court preserved the validity of past constitutional amendments and actions while declaring that Parliament would have no power in the future to amend fundamental rights.[file:41]

Hidayatullah J. concurred with the majority in result, while some judges dissented, taking the view that Parliament did possess amending power under Article 368.[file:41]

Key Takeaways:

  • Established, for the time, that fundamental rights were beyond Parliament’s amending power.[file:41]
  • Held that constitutional amendments are “law” under Article 13(2), so they cannot take away or abridge fundamental rights.[file:41]
  • Adopted prospective overruling to avoid unsettling prior constitutional and legislative developments.[file:41]
  • Strongly influenced the later constitutional debate, leading to the Twenty-fourth Amendment and eventually Kesavananda Bharati.[file:41]

Significance:

Golaknath became a landmark in Indian constitutional law because it gave fundamental rights a near-immutable status and sharply limited Parliament’s amending authority.[file:41]

Although later partly reversed by constitutional amendments and subsequent jurisprudence, the case remains central to the evolution of the basic structure doctrine and the relationship between Parliament and constitutional rights.[file:41]
