Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985)

Citation: 1986 AIR 180; 1985 Supp (2) SCR 51; (1985) 3 SCC 545[file:38]

Bench: Y.V. Chandrachud C.J., S.M. Fazal Ali, V.D. Tulzapurkar, O. Chinnappa Reddy, A. Varadarajan, JJ.[file:38]

Background:

The petitioners were pavement and slum dwellers in Bombay (including residents of Kamraj Nagar near Western Express Highway and structures near Tulsi Pipe Road), who faced forcible eviction and demolition of their hutments pursuant to a 1981 decision of the State of Maharashtra and Bombay Municipal Corporation to clear all such encroachments and deport them to their “places of origin” or outside the city.[file:38]

Earlier, in a writ petition before the Bombay High Court, some petitioners had conceded that they had no fundamental right to put up huts on pavements or public roads and undertook to vacate by 15 October 1981, after which some demolitions occurred; fresh petitions were then filed under Article 32 before the Supreme Court challenging the policy and the use of Section 314 of the Bombay Municipal Corporation Act, 1888.[file:38]

Issues:

1. Whether eviction and demolition of pavement and slum dwellers’ structures, without rehabilitation, deprives them of their means of livelihood and therefore violates the right to life under Article 21 of the Constitution.[file:38]

2. Whether Section 314 of the Bombay Municipal Corporation Act, empowering removal of encroachments “without notice”, and the impugned government and municipal actions are arbitrary, unreasonable, and violative of Articles 14, 19(1)(e), 19(1)(g), and 21.[file:38]

3. Whether the petitioners were estopped, by their earlier concession and undertakings before the High Court, from asserting fundamental rights in the Supreme Court.[file:38]

Judgement:

The Court held that there can be no estoppel against the Constitution and no individual can barter away fundamental rights; thus, prior concessions before the High Court could not prevent the petitioners from enforcing their rights under Articles 21 and 32.[file:38]

It recognised that the right to life under Article 21 includes the right to livelihood, since deprivation of livelihood would make life impossible, and held that eviction of pavement dwellers affects their livelihood and therefore engages Article 21, though their occupation of pavements does not create a legal right to encroach on public property.[file:38]

Section 314 BMC Act was upheld as constitutionally valid, but the Court read it down, holding that the discretion to remove encroachments without notice must be exercised reasonably and that, as a rule, pavement dwellers are entitled to prior notice and an opportunity to be heard before demolition, except in urgent situations.[file:38]

While ultimately permitting the removal of encroachments in public interest, the Court directed that demolitions be preceded by humane procedure, reasonable notice, and provision of alternate sites where schemes existed, balancing municipal obligations to keep public streets clear with the dwellers’ right to livelihood.[file:38]

Key Takeaways:

  • Article 21’s “right to life” was explicitly interpreted to include the right to livelihood, integrating the right to work and live with dignity for pavement and slum dwellers.[file:38]
  • No estoppel lies against enforcement of fundamental rights; undertakings or concessions in prior proceedings cannot waive or extinguish constitutional guarantees.[file:38]
  • Section 314 BMC Act is not per se unconstitutional, but the power to act “without notice” is exceptional; authorities must ordinarily give notice and follow fair procedure consistent with principles of natural justice.[file:38]
  • The Court balanced competing claims of pedestrians and pavement dwellers, affirming that no one has a fundamental right to encroach on public roads, yet insisting on humane, just, and reasonable measures in any eviction.[file:38]

Significance:

The decision became a cornerstone for socio-economic rights jurisprudence in India by firmly anchoring the right to livelihood within Article 21 and using Directive Principles as interpretive guides for fundamental rights.[file:38]

It shaped later policy and litigation on slum demolition, rehabilitation, and urban housing, compelling state authorities to reconcile urban planning objectives with constitutional protections for the urban poor.[file:38]
