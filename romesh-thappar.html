Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras (1950)

Citation: 1950 AIR 124, 1950 SCR 594[file:40]

Bench: Patanjali Sastri, Hiralal J. Kania CJ, Mehr Chand Mahajan, B.K. Mukherjea, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Saiyid Fazal Ali JJ (Fazal Ali J dissenting)[file:40]

Background:

Romesh Thappar, printer, publisher and editor of "Cross Roads", an English weekly journal published from Bombay, challenged an order dated March 1, 1950 issued by the Madras Government under Section 9(1-A) of the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1949, which banned the entry and circulation of his journal within the State of Madras.[file:40]

The ban was imposed citing the need to secure "public safety and the maintenance of public order", published in the Fort St. George Gazette, and Thappar filed directly under Article 32 claiming violation of his fundamental right to freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a).[file:40]

Issues:

1. Whether the Supreme Court could entertain a petition directly under Article 32 without first approaching the High Court under Article 226, despite concurrent jurisdiction.[file:40]

2. Whether freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) includes freedom of circulation of published material.[file:40]

3. Whether Section 9(1-A) of the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1949, authorising restrictions for "securing public safety or maintenance of public order", fell within the permissible restrictions under Article 19(2) limited to matters "undermining the security of or tending to overthrow the State".[file:40]

Judgement:

The Court overruled the preliminary objection, holding Article 32 provides a guaranteed remedy making the Supreme Court protector of fundamental rights, which cannot be refused even if High Courts have concurrent jurisdiction under Article 226.[file:40]

It affirmed that freedom of speech includes freedom of propagation of ideas ensured by freedom of circulation, citing US precedents like Ex parte Jackson that "liberty of circulation is as essential as liberty of publication".[file:40]

Section 9(1-A) was struck down as unconstitutional since "public order" and "public safety" were wider than Article 19(2)'s narrow limits of "security of State" or "overthrow of it"; the section's overbroad language made it wholly void and non-severable.[file:40]

The ban order was quashed (Fazal Ali J dissenting, holding "public safety" included State security).[file:40]

Key Takeaways:

  • Established Article 32's supremacy as guaranteed remedy for fundamental rights enforcement, rejecting exhaustion of High Court remedies prerequisite.[file:40]
  • Freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) includes freedom of circulation; banning entry prevents effective propagation of ideas.[file:40]
  • Pre-1st Amendment Article 19(2) permitted restrictions only for "security of State" or "overthrow", not broader "public order"; distinguished degrees of public disorder.[file:40]
  • Overbroad laws covering both permissible and impermissible restrictions are wholly void; no severability when abuse potential exists.[file:40]

Significance:

Landmark early free speech case adopting narrow "clear and present danger" approach, influencing First Amendment to Constitution adding "public order" grounds.[file:40]

Established judicially drawn line between State security threats justifying speech curbs versus ordinary public order maintenance left to ordinary law.[file:40]
