A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950)

Citation: 1950 AIR 27, 1950 SCR 88[cite: 5]

Bench: Hiralal J. Kania CJ, Saiyid Fazal Ali, M. Patanjali Sastri, Mehr Chand Mahajan, B.K. Mukherjea, Sudhi Ranjan Das JJ[cite: 5]

Background:

The petitioner, a communist leader, had been under varying forms of continuous detention since December 1947 under ordinary criminal charges and local public safety acts[cite: 5]. While still under custody, he was served with a fresh detention order on March 1, 1950, issued under Section 3(1) of the newly enacted Preventive Detention Act, 1950[cite: 5].

Gopalan moved a writ petition directly under Article 32 of the Constitution, filing for a writ of habeas corpus to secure his release[cite: 5]. He challenged the statutory validity of the Preventive Detention Act, claiming that its key enforcement mechanics directly violated his fundamental rights to free movement, life, and personal liberty guaranteed under Articles 13, 19, and 21[cite: 5].

Issues:

1. Whether the Preventive Detention Act, 1950, directly violates the fundamental right to move freely throughout the territory of India under Article 19(1)(d), thereby making it subject to judicial review regarding its reasonableness under Article 19(5)[cite: 5].

2. What is the precise semantic and constitutional scope of the phrase "procedure established by law" under Article 21, and whether it introduces the expansive American doctrine of procedural "due process of law" or the universal principles of natural justice[cite: 5].

3. Whether Article 22 constitutes an absolute, self-contained code governing all aspects of preventive detention, thereby completely excluding the parallel application of Articles 19 and 21[cite: 5].

Judgement:

The Supreme Court, by a 4:2 majority, upheld the constitutional validity of the Preventive Detention Act, 1950, with the sole exception of Section 14[cite: 5]. The majority (Kania CJ, Sastri, Mukherjea, and Das JJ) ruled that Article 19 has absolutely no application to a law providing for direct preventive or punitive detention[cite: 5]. The Court formulated the "directness of legislation" test, holding that Article 19(1)(d) applies only to citizens in full possession of their civil liberties; any loss of movement resulting from legal incarceration is an indirect, consequential byproduct that does not trigger a reasonableness review under Article 19(5)[cite: 5].

On Article 21, the majority rejected the petitioner's argument to equate the text with American "due process of law"[cite: 5]. The Court ruled that "law" strictly means positive, State-made or enacted law, and "procedure established by law" refers to the specific procedure laid down by a competent legislature, completely excluding unwritten principles of natural justice[cite: 5]. While Article 22 was not found to be a completely exhaustive code by all majority judges, it was held to prevail over general provisions where specific exceptions for detention were carved out[cite: 5].

However, the entire Full Court unanimously invalidated Section 14 of the Act[cite: 5]. This section made it a punishable offense for a detenu to disclose the grounds of their detention to a court of law[cite: 5]. The Court held that Section 14 directly nullified the constitutional remedies under Article 32 and the right to representation under Article 22(5)[cite: 5]. Since Section 14 was severable, its invalidation left the remainder of the Act fully operative[cite: 5]. Justices Fazal Ali and Mahajan dissented on the wider statutory structure, holding Section 12 to be ultra vires as well[cite: 5].

Key Takeaways:

  • The Single-Code / Mutual Exclusivity Theory: The rights listed in Part III are independent, watertight compartments[cite: 4, 5]. A law directly regulating one form of deprivation (such as detention under Article 22) is not required to satisfy the standard of another freedom (such as free movement under Article 19)[cite: 5].
  • Literal Lex Interpretation: "Law" under Article 21 is confined to enacted, positive statutory law[cite: 5]. The judiciary cannot read transcendental principles of natural justice or American procedural due process into the text to review the substantive wisdom of parliament[cite: 5].
  • Directness of Legislation: To determine if a fundamental right has been violated, courts must evaluate the immediate, targeted object of the statute rather than looking at its consequential or incidental effects on a citizen's lifestyle[cite: 3, 5].
  • Protection of Remedies: Even within a restrictive framework, Parliament cannot build an "iron curtain" around executive actions[cite: 5]. Laws preventing a citizen from revealing their grounds of detention to a court subvert the foundational right to constitutional remedies[cite: 5].

Significance:

As the first major fundamental rights case decided by the newly established Supreme Court of India, A.K. Gopalan laid down a strictly literal, formalistic approach to constitutional interpretation[cite: 5]. Its single-code theory and narrow restriction of personal liberty held the field for nearly three decades until it was progressively dismantled by R.C. Cooper and ultimately overruled by the landmark bench in Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India[cite: 2, 3, 5].
