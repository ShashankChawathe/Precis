R.C. Cooper v. Union of India (1970)

Citation: 1970 AIR 564, 1970 SCR (3) 530[cite: 3]

Bench: J.C. Shah, S.M. Sikri, J.M. Shelat, Vishishtha Bhargava, G.K. Mitter, C.A. Vaidialingam, K.S. Hegde, A.N. Grover, A.N. Ray, P. Jaganmohan Reddy, I.D. Dua JJ (Ray J dissenting)[cite: 3]

Background:

On July 19, 1969, the Acting President promulgated Ordinance 8 of 1969, which targeted and nationalised 14 major commercial Indian banks holding deposits of 50 crore rupees or more[cite: 3]. Before the validity of this Ordinance could be legally verified, Parliament enacted the Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act, 1969, which retrospectively replaced the Ordinance and fully vested the total assets, human resources, and business structures of these entities into corresponding new corporate banks[cite: 3].

The petitioner, Rustom Cavasjee Cooper, who was a shareholder, depositor, and director of the targeted banks, filed a writ petition under Article 32[cite: 3]. He asserted that the legislative nationalisation directly impaired his personal constitutional rights under Articles 14, 19, and 31[cite: 3]. The State contested the petition on a preliminary locus standi threshold, arguing that a corporate shareholder cannot complain about injuries caused to a separate legal company entity[cite: 3].

Issues:

1. Whether a shareholder and depositor possesses the requisite locus standi to file a petition under Article 32 if a state action simultaneously harms both their individual interests and those of the corporation[cite: 3].

2. Whether Articles 19(1)(f) and 31(2) are mutually exclusive codes, meaning that a state law providing for the compulsory acquisition of property is entirely immune from being verified for procedural or substantive reasonableness under Article 19[cite: 3].

3. Whether the statutory method provided in the Act for calculating compensation by dismembering a going concern's assets, while completely ignoring intrinsic commercial values like goodwill, satisfied the constitutional guarantee of "compensation" under Article 31(2)[cite: 3].

Judgement:

The Supreme Court, by an 10:1 majority, ruled that the petitions were maintainable[cite: 3]. The Court observed that while a company is technically a distinct legal entity, it will not deny itself jurisdiction to grant relief if the impugned State action directly impairs the fundamental freedoms of individual shareholders and constituents alongside the company[cite: 3].

On the constitutional interface of liberties, the Court categorically overruled the decades-old view established in A.K. Gopalan and Bhanji Munji, holding that Articles 19(1)(f) and 31(2) are not mutually exclusive[cite: 3]. The Court declared that the extent of a citizen's protection under the Constitution is measured strictly by the direct operation and impact of the law upon their individual rights, rather than the form or object of the State action[cite: 2, 3]. Consequently, any law authorizing compulsory acquisition must satisfy the standard of regulatory and procedural reasonableness[cite: 3].

The Court further held that while Parliament is competent to acquire a holistic corporate "undertaking" under Entry 42 of List III, the actual Act was void because it violated the guarantee of compensation under Article 31(2)[cite: 3]. Rather than valuing the active undertaking as a single unit, the Act broke it up into component parts, ignored vital commercial properties such as goodwill and long-term unexpired lease interests, and utilized irrelevant double-debiting accounting methods for asset evaluation[cite: 3]. Because the kingpin provisions of Section 4, 5, and 6 were non-severable, the Act was struck down in its entirety[cite: 3]. Justice A.N. Ray dissented, validating the nationalisation framework completely[cite: 3].

Key Takeaways:

  • The Effect and Impact Test: The validity of any legislative measure must be determined by evaluating its actual, direct operation on individual freedoms rather than accepting formalistic compliance or state declarations of benevolent policy goals[cite: 3].
  • Dismantling of the Single-Code Theory: Fundamental rights do not sit in isolated pools[cite: 2]. A law for the compulsory acquisition of property under Article 31 must remain compatible with the core guarantees of equality (Article 14) and freedom (Article 19)[cite: 3].
  • Undertaking Valuation Restrictions: When a going commercial concern is legally expropriated as a single unit, the underlying valuation principles must be relevant to computing the true worth of the entire corporate entity[cite: 3]. Omitting integral assets like goodwill yields an irrelevant calculation that fails the constitutional definition of compensation[cite: 3].
  • Hostile Discrimination Prohibited: Forcing the 14 nationalised banks into total closure from banking activities, while leaving foreign and other scheduled Indian banks free to operate, constitutes an arbitrary and hostile discrimination under Article 14[cite: 3].

Significance:

Commonly known as the landmark "Bank Nationalisation Case," R.C. Cooper sparked a paradigm shift in Indian jurisprudence by fundamentally expanding the breadth of judicial review over economic laws[cite: 2, 3]. By requiring state acquisitions to pass the strict test of fundamental rights, it directly prompted the government to subsequently pass the 25th Constitutional Amendment—setting up the historic confrontation that ultimately culminated in the formulation of the Basic Structure doctrine in Kesavananda Bharati[cite: 1, 2].
