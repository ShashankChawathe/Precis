Minerva Mills Ltd. & Ors. v. Union of India & Ors. (1980)

Citation: 1980 AIR 1789, 1981 SCR (1) 206[cite: 1]

Bench: Y.V. Chandrachud CJ, P.N. Bhagwati, A.C. Gupta, N.L. Untwalia, P.S. Kailasam JJ[cite: 1]

Background:

Minerva Mills Ltd., a textile company operating in Karnataka, had its management taken over by the Central Government in October 1971 under Section 18A of the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act, 1951, following an investigation into falling production volume[cite: 1]. The undertaking was later nationalised and fully vested in the National Textile Corporation under the provisions of the Sick Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1974[cite: 1].

The petitioners initially moved a writ petition under Article 32 to challenge the constitutional validity of the Nationalisation Act and its inclusion within the protective umbrella of the Ninth Schedule[cite: 1]. However, their challenge pivotally expanded to attack Sections 4 and 55 of the Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976[cite: 1]. These sweeping post-Emergency amendments explicitly sought to remove any and all judicial scrutiny from amendments to the Constitution and grant absolute primacy to Directive Principles over individual Fundamental Rights[cite: 1].

Issues:

1. Whether Sections 4 and 55 of the Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976, transgressed the structural limitations of the Parliament's amending power under Article 368 by altering or destroying the basic structure of the Constitution[cite: 1].

2. Whether the Directive Principles of State Policy contained in Part IV can be given complete absolute primacy over the Fundamental Rights conferred under Part III, allowing the virtual abrogation of Articles 14 and 19[cite: 1].

3. Whether a donee of a limited amending power can legitimately exercise that very power to expand its authority into an absolute and unlimited constituent force[cite: 1].

Judgement:

The Supreme Court, by a 4:1 majority (with Chandrachud CJ delivering the majority opinion), struck down Section 55 of the 42nd Amendment, declaring the newly added clauses (4) and (5) of Article 368 unconstitutional and void[cite: 1]. The Court ruled that because the Constitution conferred a limited amending power on Parliament, that limited power cannot be used to expand itself into an absolute power[cite: 1]. Clause (4) was directly invalidated because it attempted to destroy judicial review—an essential feature of the basic structure—leaving fundamental rights as mere ornaments without legal remedies[cite: 1].

The majority also struck down Section 4 of the 42nd Amendment, which had expanded Article 31C to bar challenges against any law enacted to give effect to any Directive Principle[cite: 1]. The Court emphasized that Parts III and IV are like the two wheels of a chariot, and the entire edifice of the Indian Constitution is founded upon a fine harmony and balance between them[cite: 1]. Giving an absolute, unchecked license to subvert Part III for the goals of Part IV subverts the underlying purity of means and structure[cite: 1].

Justice P.N. Bhagwati concurred completely with the majority regarding the unconstitutionality of Article 368 clauses (4) and (5)[cite: 1]. However, he dissented on Section 4, holding that the expanded Article 31C did not damage the basic structure if courts maintained the power to test whether an impugned law possessed a real and substantial connection to a Directive Principle[cite: 1].

Key Takeaways:

  • Limitations on Amending Power: A limited power of amendment is itself an essential and permanent feature of the Indian Constitution, and Parliament cannot lift its own limits to claim total sovereignty[cite: 1].
  • Judicial Review is Inviolable: The power of judicial review via Articles 32 and 226 is an integral component of the basic structure[cite: 1]. Amendments trying to turn a controlled Constitution into an uncontrolled one by barring judicial scrutiny are wholly void[cite: 1].
  • Harmony of Parts III and IV: The relationship between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles is not an adversarial hierarchy[cite: 1]. The structural balance between the two constitutes the core commitment and conscience of the Constitution[cite: 1].
  • The Golden Triangle: Articles 14, 19, and 21 form a protective golden triangle for individual liberty[cite: 1]. Withdrawing their protection completely across an uncatalogued spectrum of laws tears out the heart of democratic civil freedoms[cite: 1].

Significance:

Minerva Mills stands as a monumental landmark that firmly consolidated and applied the "Basic Structure Doctrine" first laid down in Kesavananda Bharati[cite: 1]. It successfully checked legislative overreach, restored the institutional balance of power among the State organs, and ensured that structural changes cannot be insulated from judicial review behind an untestable veil[cite: 1].
